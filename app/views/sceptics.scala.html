Scala Sceptics

How could you not like Scala?

There are many reasons you might now wan't to use Scala, as simply no specific language or framework is perfect in and of it self, and none are perfectly suited for every task or every person.

Then there's al that religious passion of whatever happens to be your belief - why it's so widespread in a world, that should boil down to fully dertermined zeros and ones, is something of a mystery.

What we wish to address in these examples are false claims and persistent myths about Scala. Some of it will be off course be our opinion, but we provide some links to both sides of the argument, and if you worry about a particular point for you business case, you should off course look into the matter.

Two red threads in this: time related critisism, that is some aspect that has now been fixed, but that googling the issue will produce a plethora of hits with this particular problem. In some cases these are refuted as easily as "Fixed in version x.x".

The other is pure tradeoff: do you need extreme runtime execution speed, maybe choose C or even Assembler. Or maybe Java hits a perfect spot for your exact challenge. Then again, if you can distribute computation, then you might prefer a language overhead to gain easy and stable distribution design. Something like Erlang or Akka.

So we'll try to get the facts straight, but you'll have to research and think for yourself before making any big decision including something as fundamental as picking a primary programming language.

There are not enough skilled Scala programmers out there?

This statement will actually mostly hold true in any expanding field, since there are none to start, and the expansion will take vacuum up everything until some critical point. It's a question of the hen and the egg to a certain degree, and it is less troublesome when the business does the pulling - eg app development, we're every other developer quickly picks up ObjectiveC for dummies. And Scala is a different beast, driven primarily by developers!

On the other hand, if you would always as a company stay with the biggest pool of potential developers, that will be the mainstream, you will compete with everyone else with the same mindset, and you will have to look for a place to get off, so you don't end up with huge installations in an out-dated technology.

The most important point though, for a new language is, that moving your developer skills into new territory, and keeping you skillset up-to-date in general, is the mark of a dedicated developer. Wether through sparetime activities or company paid time, these guys don't sit with their hands in their laps, waiting for someone else to figure out, what the next technology is. Some of them are early adopters, some are going to be the "early majority". Some of them will be stuck in a less inspiring job, where they are not allowed to experiment with new languages, at least not in any important project.

In a poll(source) , 85% of all developers said, that they would like to work in another language than their current one.

Is Scala to hard/complex/messy/big for the average developer?

No! It will probably be harder to learn for some, than others, and it certainly is a task to know all of Scala well.

But the basic contention is simply way too defeatist - eg notoriously posited by Pollak 1.

For a given project/developer team, the challenge will be to work out a reasonable standard for their usage of the language. Most teams do that with Java as well. Or with choosing libraries and tooles. There are projects that don't allow the use of generics, because it isn't well known throughout the maintenance team. Or the ternary operator, or recursive algorithms, break/continue or sideeffects in @("&") vs @("&&")...

This a normal process in any context of software development, and the good news is, there are plenty of well known ways to address it:

  • Code reviews
  • Pair Programming
  • Code camps
  • Sharing experience via forum or mail
  • Maintaining or using style and code guidelines
  • Using tools in IDE or automated build software, that checks code constructs, quality, LOC per method/class etc
  • Cycling people periodically between teams
  • And many more: participating in open source projects, courses, books, tutorials

If one or more on staff cannot keep up with the rest, that is indeed a problem, but it would likely occur in any language, and it's probably a sign, that some of the guys are just not up to task. If a couple of smart guys are leaving everyone else in the dust, you must make sure it's not just a case of "smart coders", making themselves indispensable by writing impossible to understand code. A "good" programmer, almost by definition, should write code that both the computer and every other actual programmer will have a fighting chance of understanding. Including, by the way, said good programmer coming back to the code six months later.

Which is which, and where to draw the line will be a tough call on any team, for the manager and the people involved, but it's an inherent one and not tied to any specific language or technology.

As an aside, you could in fact argue, that Scala is easier as a first language, given that it has less of the ceremony and dogmatic constructs, that dates back in time, and that newcomers won't know in advance. (Eckel) You'd start with easy little case clases and move on from there.

Dynamic typing vs static typing

Lots of good arguments out the on this one, and a huge amount of worthless flame wars too.

We believe - being Scala developers - that static typing helps us in a number of ways, that dynamic typing does not. The compiler catches errors, and gives us immediate feedback on them, errors that we then don't need to catch when testing - automatically or otherwise. It allows the IDE to provide good navigation features and deterministic refactorings, which eases cleanups in a big codebase.

Now, experienced programmers in dynamic languages may work in different ways, and be able to balance these benefits of static typing with other aspects of the trade. Or they work in a setting, that doesn't require rigorous testing of menial details. Say web development, that will be thrown out in half a year anyway. That is ok.

One point though: Just because the type system somehow irritates you, this doesn't automatically mean it's bad. Like seatbealts in a car. Make sure you choose based on solid experience and skill.

But the type system is extremely complex

The complexity basically matches the expressiveness and power of the type system. It's not completely invalid to give up on this complexity, and switch to one of the non-statically typed languages, but really, unless you are a library designer, most of the hard work has already been done in Scala.

In everyday, practical use, it just works behind the scenes, and most of the time, it's just sensible to say exactly in the code, what kind of data a given function expects, and what it returns.

Performance

Scala er et JVM sprog og performer for det meste som Java. En række features er dyrere end tilsvarende håndkode i Java (fx list.map() og tilsv. for loops modsat do/while, som performer som Java).

Det hører med i implementeringsvalg, for de fleste projekter hamrende irrelevant og minder slående om diskussionerne i overgangen fra C -> Java. Højere niveau => mere computerekraft. Det sikrer moores lov os. Den knappe resource er oftest udviklerens tids (særligt i vedligeholdelsesfasen..)
